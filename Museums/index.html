Museums

History is a very important subject. By studying the past, we learn from our ancestors, and we know what mistakes we must avoid in order to preserve freedom and our society. This is even more important in coutries like the United States, where segregation towards black people existed for a long time. However, there is not only bad things in history, and those things have to be remembered too. That is why, in New Orleans, museums are extremely developped. with more than 60 museums in the city, there is no doubt you will find the one that'll poke your interest !

New Orleans Museum of Art

The NOMA (New Orleans Museum of Art) is the oldest museum in New Orleans. It was previously named as the Delgado Museum of Art, as it was initally funded by Isaac Delgado, a local art collector and philanthropist. The museum opened its doors in 1911, but Delgado couldn't attend the ceremony because of health issues. It features no less than 40,000 objects, covering a total of 5,000 years of art history. It contains a vast majority of European artworks, alongside some American ones. Some famous artists exposed there are Monet, Renoir, Picasso, Rodin...

$15 for adults, $10 for seniors, military and LA Teachers, $8 for University students, free for the youth and children

National WWII museum

This museum is not a simple WWII museum. It has been designated by the U.S. Congress as the official national WWII museum of the United States in 2004. It opened its doors in 2000, and was primarily focused on the D-Day, with the amphibious invasion of Normandy. As the Higgins boats, that were vital to these amphibious operations, were designed, built and tested in New Orleans, the museum was naturally built in this same city. Multiple planes of WWII and a Higgins boat are in display in the museum. Multiple pavillons were also built alongside the D-Day one, to cover all the history of WWII.

McKenna Museum of African American art

African American culture is deeply rooted in New Orleans, since a long time ago. As such, it was only natural that a museum dedicated to that specific style of art was built in the city. Showcasing the art collection of Dr. Dwight McKenna, the George and Leah McKenna Museum of African American art was built in 2007, and was named after Dr. Dwight's parents.

Museum of Pharmacy

Listed on the National Register of Historic Places, this museum is an historic building located in the French Quarter of New Orleans. It covers the history of pharmacy, medication and healthcare in Louisiana. It displays old medicines as well as newer ones, ranging from voodoo potions to paracetamol, explaining the recognized and believed benefits of said medicines. It also explains the history of the first licensed pharmacist in the United States, who came from New Orleans. This man, Louis J. Dufilho Jr, passed a law that required a licensing examination for pharmacist who whished to practice their profession.

Mardi Gras World

Mardi Gras is without a doubt the most known event that takes places in New Orleans. But it is not an event specific to the city ! It is celebrated in many towns around the world. Here at Mardi Gras World, you can visit the place where the floats for the Mardi Gras parade of New Orleans are built. This is a giant warehouse, covering 300,000 square feet, where multiple floats are at display. It was created in 1984 by Blaine Kern, as a tourist attraction to give visitors a behind-the-scenes look at float building.

The Historic New Orleans Collection

The Historic New Orleans Collection (often abreviated as THNOC) is an institution established in 1966 by General and Mrs. L. Kemper Williams. They wanted to protect their collection of Louisiana materials, while still leaving it available for exhibition for the public, as well as for research. It consists of 35,000 library items, as well as no less than 150,000 photographs, prints, drawings, paintings and other visual artifacts. It covers a wide part of the history of New Orleans, starting from the Battle of New Orleans in 1815 to the life after Hurricane Katrina in 2005.

New Orleans Jazz Museum

What would be New Orleans without its jazz ? This music has been a part of the city for so long now, it wouldn't be the same without it. That is why, the Jazz Museum was created, to commemorate the music that is so deeply rooted in New Orleans. It opened in 1961, and changed ownership and location multiple times, even closing out of bankruptcy once. It contains multiple artifacts of jazz, for example Louis Armstrong's first cornet, or even the first jazz recording ever made, on a disc from 1917, as well as 12,000 photographs from the early days of jazz.

$8 for adults, $6 for students, seniors and military, free for children of age 6 and under

Voodoo Museum

New Orleans' Historic Voodoo Museum is a museum dedicated to the African diaspora religion of voodoo. It consists of two rooms containing voodoo gris-gris and other artifacts related to voodoo. It also hosts walking tours to the grave of Marie Laveau, considered as one (if not the one) of the most powerful voodoo priests in the entire world. The museum also has a voodoo priest on site, giving readings. It is a small museum, yet one of the few museums in the world entirely dedicated to voodoo.

$8 for children 12 & under, seniors (65+), students, and military. $10 for adults
